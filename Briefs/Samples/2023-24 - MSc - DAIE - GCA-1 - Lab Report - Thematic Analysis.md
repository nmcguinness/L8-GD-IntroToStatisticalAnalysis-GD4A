## Overview

This Group Continuous Assessment requires each team to design, conduct, analyse, and report on user testing for an immersive environment, extended reality experience, simulation, serious game, or related interactive prototype.

The purpose of this assessment is to evaluate your ability to use participant evidence to understand the quality of your immersive experience. The report should not simply describe that testing took place. It should use data to explain what participants did, what participants experienced, what changed between test conditions or builds, and what design knowledge the team gained from the process.

Your report must combine:

- **quantitative analysis** of numeric user-testing data;
- **thematic analysis** of coded free-text survey responses;
- clear interpretation of what the evidence means for the immersive experience;
- evidence-backed recommendations for development, refinement, or final evaluation.

Quality of insight and rigour of interpretation matter more than the quantity of data collected. A smaller dataset that is carefully cleaned, analysed, and interpreted is more valuable than a large dataset that is poorly understood.

All work must be based on your own team's user-testing data collected during the designated testing sessions.

## What This Report Is For

The report is intended to show that your team can turn participant testing into design knowledge.

You should use the report to answer questions such as:

- Did participants understand what to do?
- Did participants complete the intended task, level, or objective?
- Did the A/B condition or design change appear to affect participant behaviour or experience?
- What problems were most common?
- What did participants respond to positively?
- What issues remained after the final round of testing?
- What should the team keep, improve, fix, remove, or investigate further?

This is not a diary of the testing sessions. It is not a list of everything testers said. It is a structured analysis of the most important evidence gathered from testing.

## Testing Data

Your team should use all relevant data collected during testing. This may include:

| Data type | Examples |
| :- | :- |
| **Experience metrics** | Completion time, task success, navigation errors, interaction count, route taken, scenario completion, comfort rating, immersion rating, presence rating, usability rating, self-reported confidence, heart rate, galvanic skin response, or other sensor/telemetry values where relevant. |
| **Survey ratings** | Enjoyment, difficulty, clarity, frustration, confidence, usability, perceived fairness, visual readability, audio clarity. |
| **Free-text responses** | Comments about confusion, comfort, confidence, difficulty, technical issues, controls, spatial layout, feedback, visuals, audio, sensor feedback, or suggested changes. |
| **Observation notes** | Tester hesitation, repeated errors, missed affordances, points of confusion, unexpected participant behaviour. |
| **Build or condition information** | Stage 3 vs Stage 4, Version A vs Version B, tutorial on/off, altered difficulty, different signposting, altered scenario settings, different control or feedback system. |

## A/B Or Comparative Variable

Each team must identify one main comparative variable for analysis.

This may be:

| Comparison type | Example |
| :- | :- |
| **Stage comparison** | Stage 3 build compared with Stage 4 build. |
| **A/B comparison** | Version A compared with Version B. |
| **Design change** | Tutorial prompt enabled vs disabled. |
| **Difficulty or intensity change** | Standard crowd density vs reduced crowd density; high-stimulation scenario vs reduced-stimulation scenario; guidance off vs guidance on. |
| **Presentation change** | Original lighting/signposting vs revised lighting/signposting. |
| **Feedback change** | Minimal feedback vs enhanced audio/visual feedback. |

Your comparison should be simple, honest, and grounded in what was actually tested. Do not invent an A/B condition after the fact. If your comparison is weak or limited, acknowledge this in the report.

## Report Requirements

Your report must follow the provided lab report structure.

**Word limit:** 2,000–3,000 words.

This excludes tables, figures, references, appendices, and raw data.

**Format:** Submit as a rendered HTML or PDF document produced from a Quarto (`.qmd`) source file. The source file must also be submitted.

## Required Content

| Section | What it must contain |
| :- | :- |
| **Title & Abstract / Executive Summary** | Clear title and a standalone 150–200 word summary of purpose, method, key findings, and recommendations. This should be written last. |
| **Introduction** | Immersive experience context; purpose of the testing; scope of the testing sessions; 2–4 specific objectives; plain-English null and alternative hypotheses for the main numeric comparison. |
| **Methodology** | Test design; participants; materials; procedure; what data was collected; how the A/B or comparative condition was structured; ethical considerations; data cleaning decisions. |
| **Quantitative Results** | Summary statistics table; at least one 95% confidence interval; at least one group comparison plot; correlation matrix for 3–5 key numeric metrics; interpretation of each major figure/table. |
| **Thematic Analysis Results** | Description of how free-text responses were coded; summary of main themes; comparison of themes across stage/build/condition where possible; selected representative quotes; short interpretation of what the comments reveal. |
| **Discussion** | Integrated interpretation of numeric and thematic findings; 3–5 key insights; connection to testing objectives; evidence-backed recommendations; honest limitations. |
| **Conclusion** | Concise summary of what was learned, what this means for the immersive experience, and what should happen next. |
| **References** | Consistent citation format, APA recommended. |
| **Appendices** | Raw data, coded thematic analysis spreadsheet, survey instrument, analysis files, supplementary visualisations. |

## Quantitative Analysis Requirements

Your quantitative analysis must include:

- a summary table with `n`, mean or median, and spread for each group/build/condition;
- at least one 95% confidence interval reported in experience terms;
- at least one group comparison plot, such as a boxplot or equivalent;
- a correlation matrix for 3–5 relevant numeric metrics;
- 2–4 total figures supporting the report narrative;
- interpretation of each major result in plain English.

Numeric findings should be written in terms of the participant experience, not just statistical language.

For example, do not only write:

> The mean completion time was lower in Version B.

Instead, explain what this suggests:

> Players completed Version B more quickly on average, which may suggest that the revised signposting helped them understand the route through the level more efficiently.

## Thematic Analysis Requirements

Your team must complete a thematic analysis of the free-text survey responses using the provided spreadsheet.

The spreadsheet is used to convert participant comments into structured design knowledge. Each row should represent **one meaningful comment**. If a tester gives two separate points in the same answer, split the response into two rows and keep the same tester ID and question.

### Required Coding Fields

Your coded spreadsheet must include the following fields:

| Field | Purpose |
| :- | :- |
| `response_id` | Unique identifier for the comment. |
| `tester_id` | Anonymous tester identifier. Do not include student names. |
| `stage` / `version` / `condition` | Identifies the build, stage, or A/B condition associated with the response. |
| `question` | The survey question that produced the response. |
| `response` | The original free-text response. |
| `primary_code` | The main theme in the comment. |
| `secondary_code` | A more specific sub-theme, if useful. |
| `sentiment` | Positive, Neutral, Negative, or Mixed. |
| `severity` | Low, Medium, or High. |
| `design_area` | The part of the immersive experience affected. |
| `action_required` | What the team should do with this evidence. |
| `participant_impact` | How the comment affected or described the participant experience. |
| `recommendation` | A short design response or implication. |
| `include_quote` | Whether the comment is useful as a representative quote in the report. |

### What The Thematic Analysis Must Show

Your report must include:

- the number of free-text comments coded;
- a short explanation of your coding process;
- a theme summary table;
- a comparison of themes across stage/build/condition where possible;
- a summary of positive feedback;
- a summary of issues or high-severity comments;
- 2–4 short representative quotes;
- an explanation of how the themes informed your recommendations.

Do not include long lists of raw comments in the main report. Raw comments and the coded spreadsheet should be placed in the appendices or submitted as supporting files.

## Using AI To Support Thematic Analysis

You may use an AI assistant to help summarise or organise your completed coded spreadsheet, provided this is allowed under the institute policy and is acknowledged appropriately.

However, the coding must remain your responsibility. The team must read the comments, decide what they mean, and check that any AI-supported summary is accurate and fair.

AI must not be used to invent data, exaggerate findings, or replace your own interpretation.

A suitable use of AI would be:

> We coded the comments manually using the provided spreadsheet, then used an AI assistant to help summarise theme counts and draft possible interpretations. The final interpretation was checked and edited by the team.

An unsuitable use would be:

> We pasted raw comments into an AI assistant and accepted its analysis without checking or coding the data ourselves.

## Appendix B: Thematic Analysis Guidance

Thematic analysis in this assessment means turning free-text participant comments into structured design knowledge.

It does **not** mean counting words only. A word frequency table may be useful as supporting evidence, but it is not a substitute for reading, coding, and interpreting what participants said.

### Example Coded Comment

| response_id | stage | question | response | primary_code | secondary_code | sentiment | severity | design_area | action_required | participant_impact | recommendation | include_quote |
| :- | :- | :- | :- | :- | :- | :- | :- | :- | :- | :- | :- | :- |
| R014 | Stage 4 | What confused you? | The crowd density changed but I did not realise I was meant to use the breathing prompt. | Clarity | Coping Prompt | Negative | Medium | Interaction Design | Improve | Caused Confusion | Add clearer visual and audio feedback when the coping prompt becomes available. | Yes |

### Example Report Interpretation

> Free-text responses were manually coded into themes using the supplied thematic analysis spreadsheet. The most common issue was clarity, particularly around scenario changes and coping-prompt feedback. Although Stage 4 comments contained fewer references to confusion than Stage 3, several testers still reported missing important changes in the environment. This suggests that the revised build improved participant understanding, but that scenario feedback should be made more explicit in the final version.

```r

ggplot(theme_counts, aes(x = primary_code, y = n, fill = stage)) +
  geom_col(position = "dodge") +
  coord_flip() +
  labs(
    title = "Free-text Comment Themes by Testing Stage",
    x = "Theme",
    y = "Number of comments"
  )
```
